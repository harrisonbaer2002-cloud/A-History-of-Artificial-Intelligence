Project Overview  ·  Motivation & Goals

Motivation & Goals

Artificial intelligence is one of the most innovative and quickly evolving technologies present day. Understanding its history could help in giving context to how it has become the technology we possess today. Mapping out this growth can help indicate what future artificial intelligence will be capable of. The setbacks and failures of past and present artificial intelligence are also important to know.

The primary goal of this project was to trace the history of the evolution of artificial intelligence.

Specifically, major eras where breakthroughs or setbacks were encountered, then analyze how each of these affected each other. In the end, the presentation would give a user-friendly interface to learn about this topic.

01
1936 – 1950  ·  Introduction

The Foundation

The history of artificial intelligence began with a question: could a machine be capable of simulating any algorithm? In 1936, British mathematician Alan Turing sought to answer this notion, setting in stone the foundation of modern computing. Turing's ideas came to fruition during World War II, where his device was used to break Nazi Germany's Enigma codes at Bletchley Park, England. His team's success demonstrated that machines could tackle many complex problems faster than a human. The result posed another question to Turing: if machines could outthink human's in one area, could they be made to reason and learn like the human mind?

"Could a machine be capable of simulating any algorithm?"

Turing published his answer in 1950, in a paper titled "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." It introduced what he called the "Imitation Game". This was a test to determine whether a machine could exhibit intelligent behavior indistinguishable from a human. The notion later was known as the Turing Test, and it became the earliest benchmark for artificial intelligence. Researchers today still debate over the question over if machines will ever reach this level of intelligence.

02
1956  ·  Background

The Birth of a Field

In the summer of 1956, a group of scientists gathered at Dartmouth College in New Hampshire that eventually established Artificial Intelligence as a field of study. The two month long workshop took place in Hanover, New Hampshire. The conference officially coined the term "artificial intelligence" for the first time. The goal of the conference was to explore the notion of whether a machine could practice learning and intelligence like humans do. Notable researchers like John McCarthy, Claude Shannon, and Marvin Minsky believed that in principle, human intelligence could be simulated by a machine.

The conference gave the field a name and an identity. The idea of artificial intelligence was born.

However through their conference it was determined this technology take years if not decades to develop. Although no major breakthroughs took place, the impact the conference had was lasting. It brought together a team of renowned scientists and gave the field a name and an identity. The idea of artificial intelligence was born, and scientists were steadfast in their pursuit of this technology.

03
1973 – 1980s  ·  Stagnation

The AI Winters

Following the Dartmouth conference, ambition in AI research exploded in the 1960's. Researchers predicted machines would harness human level intelligence in the coming decades. However this notion would die down in the coming years. In 1973 a report done by the British government called the Lighthill Report concluded research failed to live up to the previous prediction. This report triggered a sizable pull in funding to the AI field, the event later known as the First AI Winter. A brief comeback occurred in the 1980's through the lens of expert systems. However their cost was too great and the market for them collapsed. This was later known as the Second AI Winter.

The collapse of the expert system market marked the end of a second period of optimism in artificial intelligence.

Expert systems were programs designed to mimic the decisions of human specialist (fields such as medicine and finance). They relied on rule based logic which required teams of engineers to manually encode knowledge into the system. The scale of work turned out to be too large, and the rules became too complex and costly to maintain. The collapse of the expert system market marked the end of a second period of optimism in artificial intelligence.

04
1940s – 2014  ·  Breakthrough

Deep Learning

The concept of a neural network stretched back to the 1940's. However, limited computing power made it impractical for decades. Progress resumed in the 1980's through the work of Yann LeCun, when he developed Convolutional Neural Networks that could processed images by detecting features layer by layer. However datasets of the era were too small for the architecture's potential to be seen. That changed in 2012, when researchers Alex Krizhevsky and Geoffrey Hinton entered a convolutional network called AlexNet into the ImageNet challenge. It was a global competition to classify images across one thousand categories. AlexNet achieved a top-5 error rate of 15.3 percent, well ahead of the runner-up's 26.2 percent. The event was later known as the breakthrough that launched the modern deep learning era.

For sequential data like language and audio, researchers had developed Recurrent Neural Networks. These carried a hidden state forward through each step of a sequence to model what came before. However a flaw called the vanishing gradient made long sequences difficult to learn. Sepp Hochreiter and Jürgen Schmidhuber addressed this in 1997 with Long Short-Term Memory networks. They used gated mechanisms to selectively retain or discard information. And a further development came in 2014, when Ian Goodfellow introduced Generative Adversarial Networks. They developed a framework in which two networks competed against each other to produce increasingly realistic synthetic content. This raised concerns about fabricated media that still persist today.

05
2017 – Present  ·  Revolution

The Modern Era of AI

Following the deep learning era, researchers turned their attention to language. Early language models relied on statistical methods that predicting the next word based on the few that came before it. However these models had no real understanding of context. But progress resumed through Recurrent Neural Networks. The networks carried information forward through a sequence to inform the next step. However a flaw called the vanishing gradient made long sequences difficult to train on. That changed in 2017, when researchers at Google published a paper titled "Attention Is All You Need." It introduced the transformer architecture that could process all words in a sequence simultaneously. This allowed models to capture context far more effectively than anything before.

It has become known as the moment artificial intelligence entered mainstream culture.

The following year, OpenAI released GPT-1 and Google released BERT. They were the first large language models built on the transformer architecture. In this release, neither made a significant public impact. That changed in 2020, when OpenAI released GPT-3 that was trained on 175 billion parameters. The model demonstrated that scale alone could produce coherent writing and multi-step reasoning. The event was later known as the turning point for large language models as a serious technology. Further development came in 2022 when OpenAI released ChatGPT to the public. It was the first time millions of users interacted directly with a language model. It has become known as the moment artificial intelligence entered mainstream culture. Despite their capabilities these models carry significant limitations. They tend to generate factually incorrect information with confidence, a problem known as hallucination. Their knowledge remains fixed at a training cutoff which leaving recent events outside their awareness.

06
Present Day  ·  Critical Perspective

Limitations of Large
Language Models

At the moment, most AI systems are considered "narrow AI." They are good at specific jobs, can be bad at others, and produce errors in their responses. That's not to say large language models like Chat GPT are not generational innovations. They can write essays, answer questions, and help code. Because of this, many people think they might be an early form of AGI. Large Language Models are trained on huge amounts of text and learn through patterns in language. This is not the same process as humans go through. The LLMs may seem convincing and accurate, but they often make mistakes. Some studies show that LLMs can reason well in general, but often fail in specialized areas. E-commerce recommendations can be unreliable unless they are combined with other models or tools. Their answers are given with confidence, although sometimes incorrect, and can also struggle with logic and common sense. This raises the question of whether they are truly intelligent or just trained at copying human language.

Large Language Models (LLMs) are AI systems trained on massive amounts of text to identify patterns and generate human-like language. They can perform diverse tasks, such as answering questions, writing essays, and coding. Despite their capabilities, LLMs are limited by their training data and statistical prediction mechanisms, which means they do not reason like humans and can produce errors or illogical outputs. Recent studies highlight the distinction between understanding and prediction. While LLMs generate coherent language, they often lack true comprehension of the content. They can appear intelligent but may fail in specialized areas or in tasks requiring real-world grounding. Multimodal models, which combine text with images or audio, have been proposed as a method to improve reasoning and approach more human-like understanding.

Project Overview  ·  Conclusion

Results

The progress towards artificial intelligence was not a fully linear one. The field encountered a multitude of setbacks in its growth and funding at times, slowing the pace of its evolution. The work towards Large Language Models we have access to today took over 80 years to develop. Despite their capabilities, these LLMs have fundamental limitations and flaws.
